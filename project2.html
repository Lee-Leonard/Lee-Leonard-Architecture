Proposal Overview

This project proposes an architectural response to Northern Ireland’s household water crisis through integrated rainwater harvesting systems. Using pitched roofs, rain gardens, and decentralised filtration, the design creates a self-sustaining water network for residential and communal use. It reduces reliance on central infrastructure while enhancing ecological, social, and aesthetic value. Water features change with the seasons, encouraging public interaction and awareness. By combining function with form, the proposal offers a replicable model that turns water management into an opportunity for resilient, sustainable community development grounded in ecological design principles.

The project is set on the former Scirocco Works site in Belfast, a 16.5-acre brownfield area with a rich industrial past situated along the edge of the River Lagan. Historically home to various glassworks, clay makers, and engineering factories, the site has a unique character within the context of Belfast. With a rich history of crafts and art-based trades, this roots its legacy within the fabric of Belfast.

Now vacant, the site offers an opportunity for regeneration. This proposal aims to transform this abandoned land into an artist-led, ecologically focused, and hydrologically self-sustained community. The historical significance informed the decision to regenerate this location for artists by providing housing and studio spaces; an attempt to pay homage to the sites rich tapestry, creating a hub for creativity where it was once so evident. The sites flat terrain, strong transport links, and striking views -towards the River Lagan and H&W Titanic Cranes-make it ideal for a sustainable redevelopment that blends architecture, nature, and cultural identity.

Design Approach

This design draws inspiration from traditional Belfast terraced housing, reflecting the city’s industrial heritage through linear rows that subtly shift to mimic a river’s flow. These gentle twists enhance rainwater movement across the site while encouraging exploration. The layout integrates green spaces, rain gardens, and water features, leading to the River Lagan and evoking the idea of a new ‘hidden river’ within Belfast. Strategically located and well-connected, the site acts as an urban catchment method-drawing people in, promoting movement, and fostering community interaction. It becomes a place of connection, blending social, ecological, and historical narratives.
